Not all crack sealers are created equal. Using the wrong product for your climate can mean failed seals, wasted labor, and unhappy clients. Here's how to choose.

## Step 1: Understand Your Climate

### Hot Climates (Year-round >25°C)
- **Risk**: Sealant softening and tracking in summer heat
- **Solution**: High-softening-point hot-applied sealant (≥90°C softening point)
- **Avoid**: Low-softening-point products that become sticky on hot pavement

### Cold Climates (Winter <-20°C)
- **Risk**: Sealant becoming brittle and losing adhesion
- **Solution**: Polymer-modified sealants with low-temperature flexibility specs
- **Key spec**: Look for bond strength retention after freeze-thaw cycling

### Freeze-Thaw Zones (Wide temperature swings)
- **Risk**: Both summer softening AND winter cracking
- **Solution**: Premium polymer-modified formulations with proven field history
- **Key spec**: Flow resistance at 60°C AND low-temperature flexibility at -30°C

## Step 2: Match Product to Crack Type

| Crack Type | Width | Recommended Product |
|------------|-------|-------------------|
| Hairline | <3 mm | Sealcoat or fog seal (not worth individual sealing) |
| Narrow | 3–12 mm | Cold pour filler (DIY-friendly) |
| Medium | 12–25 mm | Hot-applied sealant or cold pour filler |
| Wide | 25–50 mm | Hot-applied with backer rod |
| Very wide / alligator | >50 mm | Remove and patch — sealing won't help |

## Step 3: Consider Traffic and Budget

- **High-traffic commercial**: Hot-applied, fuel-resistant, high flow resistance
- **Residential driveways**: Cold pour filler or sealing tape (DIY-friendly)
- **Budget-constrained municipalities**: Cold pour for initial treatment, hot-applied for high-priority roads

## The Cost of Getting It Wrong

A $2/bag cold pour filler used on a 40 mm crack on a truck route will fail in weeks. A $25/box hot-applied sealant used on a residential driveway is overkill. The right product for the right application saves money on both material AND rework.
