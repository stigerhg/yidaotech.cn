The debate between cold mix and hot mix asphalt for repairs has been going on for decades — but in recent years, the balance has tipped decisively toward cold mix. Here's why.

## The Hot Mix Problem

Hot mix asphalt is the gold standard for new pavement construction, but for repairs it has three fundamental problems:

1. **Temperature dependency** — Hot mix must be placed at 135–160°C. Once it cools, workability drops sharply. This limits effective working time to 30–45 minutes.
2. **Plant dependency** — You need a hot mix plant within reasonable hauling distance. Small jobs become uneconomical when minimum order quantities are 10+ tons.
3. **Seasonal limitations** — Most hot mix plants shut down in winter. That's exactly when freeze-thaw potholes are at their worst.

## Cold Mix Advantages

Modern polymer-modified cold mix asphalt addresses all three:

| Factor | Hot Mix | Cold Mix |
|--------|---------|----------|
| Minimum job size | 10+ tons | 1 bag (25 kg) |
| Application temperature | 135–160°C | -20°C to +40°C |
| Shelf life | Hours (once cooled) | 12+ months sealed |
| Equipment needed | Paver, roller, plant | Hand tamper or plate |
| Winter availability | Usually unavailable | Year-round |

## Performance Reality

The old perception that cold mix is a "temporary fix" comes from early-generation products that used low-quality cutbacks. Modern cold mix uses engineered binders that cure to performance levels approaching hot mix — and in some conditions (wet, cold, thin lifts), cold mix actually outperforms hot mix because it doesn't cool and stiffen during compaction.

## Bottom Line

For maintenance crews doing distributed repairs (utility cuts, potholes, edge breaks), cold mix is the clear winner. The ability to keep a pallet on the truck and do repairs on demand — without coordinating with a hot plant — more than justifies the slightly higher per-ton material cost.
